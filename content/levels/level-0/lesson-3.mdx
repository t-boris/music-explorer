# How We Hear Pitch

Our ears do not perceive frequency on a linear scale. The difference between 100 Hz and 200 Hz sounds like the same "distance" as the difference between 1000 Hz and 2000 Hz -- even though the second gap is ten times larger in absolute terms. Understanding this **<DigDeeper>logarithmic</DigDeeper> perception** is the key to understanding why music is organized the way it is.

## Logarithmic Perception

Doubling any frequency produces the same perceptual result: an **<DigDeeper>octave</DigDeeper>**. This is true regardless of the starting frequency:

```
55 Hz  -> 110 Hz  = one octave  (A1 to A2)
110 Hz -> 220 Hz  = one octave  (A2 to A3)
220 Hz -> 440 Hz  = one octave  (A3 to A4)
440 Hz -> 880 Hz  = one octave  (A4 to A5)
```

Each octave spans a larger range of frequencies, yet they all sound like the "same step." This is because human hearing responds to **ratios**, not differences. A ratio of 2:1 always sounds like an octave, whether you start at 50 Hz or 5000 Hz.

> **Key concept:** Our perception of pitch is **logarithmic** -- we hear equal ratios as equal distances. This is why musical scales are built on multiplicative relationships, not additive ones.

## Equal Temperament: Dividing the Octave

Western music divides each octave into **12 equal <DigDeeper>semitone</DigDeeper>s**. This system, called **12-tone <DigDeeper>equal temperament</DigDeeper>** (12-TET), ensures that every semitone has the same frequency ratio. Since 12 semitones must multiply together to produce a ratio of 2:1 (one octave), each semitone's ratio is:

```
semitone ratio = 2^(1/12) ~ 1.05946

To go up n semitones from frequency f:
f_new = f * 2^(n/12)
```

This formula is the mathematical backbone of the modern Western tuning system. On a guitar, **each fret raises the pitch by exactly one semitone** -- which means moving 12 frets up always doubles the frequency.

**Example: calculating note frequencies from A4 = 440 Hz**

| Semitones from A4 | Note | Calculation | Frequency |
|-------------------|------|-------------|-----------|
| 0 | A4 | 440 * 2^(0/12) | 440.00 Hz |
| 2 | B4 | 440 * 2^(2/12) | 493.88 Hz |
| 3 | C5 | 440 * 2^(3/12) | 523.25 Hz |
| 5 | D5 | 440 * 2^(5/12) | 587.33 Hz |
| 7 | E5 | 440 * 2^(7/12) | 659.26 Hz |
| 12 | A5 | 440 * 2^(12/12) | 880.00 Hz |

## Why A4 = 440 Hz?

The choice of **A4 = 440 Hz** as the universal tuning reference is a convention, not a law of physics. It was standardized internationally in 1955, though its use had been growing since the 19th century.

Historically, tuning varied dramatically:

- **Baroque era:** A4 ~ 415 Hz (about a semitone lower than modern)
- **Classical era:** A4 ~ 425-435 Hz
- **Modern standard:** A4 = 440 Hz
- **Some orchestras today:** A4 = 442-445 Hz (slightly brighter sound)

For guitar, the standard tuning of the six open strings (low to high) produces these frequencies:

```
String    Note    Frequency
──────────────────────────
6th (E2)   E2     82.41 Hz
5th (A2)   A2    110.00 Hz
4th (D3)   D3    146.83 Hz
3rd (G3)   G3    196.00 Hz
2nd (B3)   B3    246.94 Hz
1st (E4)   E4    329.63 Hz
```

## The Audible Range

The human ear can detect frequencies roughly between **20 Hz and 20,000 Hz** (<DigDeeper term="audible range">20 kHz</DigDeeper>), though the upper limit decreases with age. Musical instruments occupy a subset of this range:

- **Bass guitar lowest note (B0):** ~31 Hz
- **Guitar lowest note (E2):** ~82 Hz
- **Guitar highest practical note (~24th fret, E string):** ~1319 Hz
- **Piano full range:** 27.5 Hz to 4186 Hz
- **Human voice:** roughly 80 Hz to 1100 Hz (fundamentals)

Most of the guitar's fundamental frequencies fall between 82 Hz and 1320 Hz, but the overtones extend much higher -- which is why the tone knob on a guitar affects brightness by filtering high-frequency harmonics.

## The Tradeoff of Equal Temperament

Equal temperament is a mathematical compromise. The **pure** intervals from the harmonic series (3:2 for a fifth, 5:4 for a major third) are slightly different from their equal-tempered equivalents:

```
Interval        Pure Ratio    ET Ratio       Difference
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Perfect fifth   3:2 = 1.5000  2^(7/12) = 1.4983   -0.11%
Major third     5:4 = 1.2500  2^(4/12) = 1.2599   +0.79%
```

The differences are small but audible to trained ears. The benefit of equal temperament is that **every key sounds equally in tune** -- you can play in any key and transpose freely. Without it, changing keys on a fixed-pitch instrument would require retuning.

## From the Stage

<MusicStory
  title="The Great Tuning War"
  era="1939"
  connection="The A=440 Hz standard you use to tune your guitar was the result of decades of debate — proof that even 'obvious' musical conventions required international negotiation."
>

For centuries, there was no universal agreement on what frequency the note A should be. In the Baroque era, A was typically around 415 Hz — nearly a semitone lower than today's standard. By the 19th century, orchestras had entered an unofficial **pitch arms race**: conductors kept raising the tuning to make their ensembles sound brighter and more brilliant. Some opera houses in Italy tuned as high as A=450 Hz, which strained singers' voices and caused real physical harm.

In 1939, an international conference in London finally standardized **A4 = 440 Hz** as the reference pitch. Even this was a compromise — the French had been pushing for 435 Hz (their national standard since 1859), while many German and Austrian orchestras preferred 443-445 Hz. The 440 Hz standard was proposed as a middle ground that could satisfy everyone.

Today, 440 Hz is nearly universal, but the debate never fully ended. Many period-instrument ensembles still tune to 415 Hz for Baroque music, and some modern orchestras (notably in Vienna and Berlin) tune to 442-443 Hz for a slightly brighter sound. Every time you tune your guitar to A=440, you are participating in a convention that took centuries of argument to establish.

</MusicStory>
