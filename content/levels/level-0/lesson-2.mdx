# Harmonics & Overtones

When you pluck a guitar string, it does not vibrate at a single frequency. The string simultaneously produces a **fundamental frequency** (the lowest and loudest pitch) plus a series of higher frequencies called **overtones** or **harmonics**. This phenomenon shapes the very sound of music.

## The Harmonic Series

The **harmonic series** is the set of frequencies produced by a vibrating string. Each harmonic is an integer multiple of the fundamental:

```
Harmonic    Ratio    Frequency (A2 = 110 Hz)    Note
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1st (fund)  1x       110 Hz                     A2
2nd         2x       220 Hz                     A3
3rd         3x       330 Hz                     E4
4th         4x       440 Hz                     A4
5th         5x       550 Hz                     C#5
6th         6x       660 Hz                     E5
7th         7x       770 Hz                     ~G5
8th         8x       880 Hz                     A5
```

> **Key insight:** The harmonic series is built into the physics of every vibrating string. The intervals between harmonics -- the **octave** (2:1), **perfect fifth** (3:2), **perfect fourth** (4:3), and **major third** (5:4) -- are the physical foundation of Western harmony.

## Why Instruments Sound Different: Timbre

If a guitar and a piano both play A4 (440 Hz), why do they sound completely different? The answer is **timbre** (pronounced "TAM-ber") -- the unique "color" or "texture" of a sound.

Timbre is determined by the **relative amplitudes** of the harmonics present in a sound:

- **Guitar:** Strong fundamental, prominent 2nd and 3rd harmonics, gradually fading higher harmonics. The attack has a complex burst of frequencies that quickly settles.
- **Piano:** Rich set of harmonics with a percussive attack and long sustain. The stiff steel strings produce slightly **inharmonic** overtones (not perfect integer multiples).
- **Flute:** Nearly pure tone -- dominant fundamental with very weak upper harmonics.
- **Distorted guitar:** Clipping adds strong odd-numbered harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th), which is why distortion sounds "thicker" and more aggressive.

The formula for the frequency of the nth harmonic is simply:

```
f_n = n * f_1

where f_1 is the fundamental frequency
and n = 1, 2, 3, 4, ...
```

## Natural Harmonics on Guitar

You can isolate individual harmonics on a guitar by lightly touching the string at specific **node points** -- positions where a particular harmonic's standing wave has zero displacement.

| Fret | String Division | Harmonic | Interval Above Fundamental |
|------|----------------|----------|---------------------------|
| 12th | 1/2 | 2nd | Octave |
| 7th | 1/3 | 3rd | Octave + perfect fifth |
| 5th | 1/4 | 4th | Two octaves |
| 4th (near) | 1/5 | 5th | Two octaves + major third |
| 3rd (near) | 1/6 | 6th | Two octaves + perfect fifth |

**How to play a natural harmonic:**

1. Lightly touch the string directly above the **fret wire** (not between frets as in normal playing)
2. Pluck the string with your picking hand
3. Immediately lift your fretting finger -- the harmonic will ring out as a clear, bell-like tone

The 12th fret harmonic divides the string exactly in half, producing a pitch one octave above the open string. The 7th fret harmonic divides it into thirds, producing a pitch an octave and a fifth above the fundamental.

## The Frequency Ratios That Define Music

The harmonic series gives us the fundamental frequency ratios of Western music:

```
Octave:         2:1  (2nd harmonic / 1st)
Perfect fifth:  3:2  (3rd harmonic / 2nd)
Perfect fourth: 4:3  (4th harmonic / 3rd)
Major third:    5:4  (5th harmonic / 4th)
Minor third:    6:5  (6th harmonic / 5th)
```

These ratios are not arbitrary cultural choices -- they emerge directly from physics. The simpler the ratio, the more **consonant** (harmonically stable) the interval sounds. This connection between mathematics and musical perception is one of the oldest discoveries in science, dating back to **Pythagoras** (6th century BCE).

Understanding the harmonic series is essential because it explains **why** certain note combinations sound good together -- a question we will answer precisely when we study intervals and consonance in Level 2.
