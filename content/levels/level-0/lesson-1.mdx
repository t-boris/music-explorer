# What is Sound?

Sound is a **vibration** that travels through a medium (air, water, or solid material) as a wave. When an object vibrates, it creates pressure changes in the surrounding air that our ears detect and our brains interpret as sound.

## Frequency and Pitch

The **frequency** of a sound wave determines its **pitch** -- how high or low we perceive it. Frequency is measured in **Hertz (Hz)**, which represents the number of wave cycles per second.

- **Low frequency** (e.g., 80 Hz) = low pitch (bass guitar, kick drum)
- **High frequency** (e.g., 4000 Hz) = high pitch (piccolo, cymbal shimmer)

> The human ear can typically detect frequencies between **20 Hz and 20,000 Hz**. Musical instruments generally produce fundamentals in the range of about 27 Hz (lowest piano key) to 4186 Hz (highest piano key).

## Amplitude and Volume

The **amplitude** of a sound wave determines its **volume** (loudness). Greater amplitude means a louder sound. Volume is measured in **decibels (dB)**.

| Source | Approximate dB |
|--------|---------------|
| Whisper | 30 dB |
| Normal conversation | 60 dB |
| Electric guitar (amplified) | 90-110 dB |
| Rock concert | 110-120 dB |

## The Overtone Series

When a guitar string vibrates, it doesn't just vibrate at one frequency. It produces a **fundamental frequency** plus a series of **overtones** (harmonics) at integer multiples of the fundamental:

```
Fundamental:  110 Hz  (A2)
2nd harmonic: 220 Hz  (A3 - one octave up)
3rd harmonic: 330 Hz  (E4 - octave + fifth)
4th harmonic: 440 Hz  (A4 - two octaves up)
5th harmonic: 550 Hz  (C#5 - two octaves + major third)
```

This overtone series is the physical basis for **intervals**, **chords**, and **harmony** -- concepts we'll explore in depth in later levels.
