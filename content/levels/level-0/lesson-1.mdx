# What is Sound?

Sound is a **vibration** that travels through a medium -- air, water, or solid material -- as a pressure wave. When you pluck a guitar string, the string oscillates back and forth, pushing and pulling the surrounding air molecules. These pressure changes propagate outward as a **sound wave** that eventually reaches your ear.

## The Anatomy of a Wave

Every sound wave has three fundamental properties:

- **Frequency** -- the number of complete wave cycles per second, measured in **Hertz (Hz)**
- **Amplitude** -- the height of the wave, which determines **loudness**
- **Wavelength** -- the physical distance between two identical points on consecutive cycles

```
        Amplitude
           |
     +     v     +
    / \         / \
   /   \       /   \        wavelength
--/-----\-----/-----\----   <---------->
         \   /       \   /
          \ /         \ /
           -           -

  |--- one cycle ---|
       = 1/frequency
```

The relationship between these properties follows a simple formula:

```
speed of sound = frequency x wavelength
v = f x lambda

In air at room temperature: v ~ 343 m/s
```

## Frequency and Pitch

**Frequency** directly determines **pitch** -- how high or low we perceive a sound. A faster vibration (higher frequency) produces a higher pitch; a slower vibration (lower frequency) produces a lower pitch.

On guitar, this relationship is intuitive:

- **Thicker strings** vibrate more slowly, producing **lower pitches** (the low E string vibrates at about 82 Hz)
- **Thinner strings** vibrate more quickly, producing **higher pitches** (the high E string vibrates at about 330 Hz)
- **Fretting a note** shortens the vibrating portion of the string, raising the frequency and pitch

> **Key concept:** The pitch of a vibrating string depends on its **length**, **tension**, and **mass per unit length**. The formula is:
>
> `f = (1 / 2L) * sqrt(T / mu)`
>
> where `L` is the string length, `T` is the tension, and `mu` is the linear density.

## Amplitude and Volume

**Amplitude** determines how loud a sound is, measured in **decibels (dB)**. When you strum a guitar hard, the string swings through a wider arc (greater amplitude), displacing more air and creating a louder sound.

| Source | Approximate Level |
|--------|------------------|
| Whisper | 30 dB |
| Normal conversation | 60 dB |
| Acoustic guitar (strumming) | 70-80 dB |
| Electric guitar (amplified) | 90-110 dB |
| Rock concert | 110-120 dB |

> **Important:** The decibel scale is **logarithmic**. Every increase of 10 dB represents roughly a **doubling** of perceived loudness. A 100 dB signal is not "twice as loud" as 50 dB -- it is perceived as approximately **32 times louder**.

## Standing Waves on a Guitar String

When you pluck a guitar string, the wave reflects off the fixed endpoints (the nut and the bridge) and creates a **standing wave**. The string vibrates in a pattern where certain points, called **nodes**, remain stationary while other points, called **antinodes**, oscillate with maximum amplitude.

The simplest standing wave pattern -- the **fundamental** -- has nodes only at the two endpoints and one antinode at the center. This is the lowest frequency the string can produce and defines the note you hear. Higher-order standing wave patterns (harmonics) divide the string into 2, 3, 4, or more equal segments -- a topic we explore in the next lesson.

Understanding these physical principles is the foundation for everything that follows: intervals, scales, chords, and harmony all emerge from the mathematics of vibrating strings.
