# The Staff & Clefs

Written music uses a visual system to represent pitch and rhythm on paper. The foundation of this system is the **staff** -- a set of five horizontal lines and four spaces, each representing a specific pitch.

## The Five-Line Staff

The **staff** (plural: staves) consists of five parallel lines. Notes are placed either **on** a line or **in** a space between lines. Higher positions on the staff represent higher pitches; lower positions represent lower pitches.

```
───────────────────  (line 5)
                     (space 4)
───────────────────  (line 4)
                     (space 3)
───────────────────  (line 3)
                     (space 2)
───────────────────  (line 2)
                     (space 1)
───────────────────  (line 1)
```

Without a **clef**, these lines and spaces have no assigned note names. The clef is the symbol at the beginning of the staff that anchors specific pitches to specific positions.

## Treble Clef (G Clef)

The **treble clef** is the most common clef and the one guitarists read. Its curl wraps around **line 2**, designating it as **G4** (the G above middle C, 392 Hz).

From this anchor point, the notes on each line and space are:

```
Lines (bottom to top):     E4 - G4 - B4 - D5 - F5
Mnemonic:                  Every Good Boy Does Fine

Spaces (bottom to top):    F4 - A4 - C5 - E5
Mnemonic:                  F-A-C-E (spells "face")
```

> **Guitar-specific note:** Guitar music is written in treble clef, but sounds **one octave lower** than written. This convention (called **octave transposition**) keeps guitar music comfortably on the staff without excessive ledger lines. When you see middle C on guitar sheet music, you actually play C3, not C4.

## Bass Clef (F Clef)

The **bass clef** anchors **F3** (the F below middle C) on **line 4**. It is used for instruments with lower ranges like bass guitar, cello, and the left hand of piano. Two dots surround line 4 to mark the F.

```
Lines (bottom to top):     G2 - B2 - D3 - F3 - A3
Mnemonic:                  Good Boys Do Fine Always

Spaces (bottom to top):    A2 - C3 - E3 - G3
Mnemonic:                  All Cows Eat Grass
```

While electric guitarists primarily read treble clef, understanding bass clef is valuable for reading bass lines, piano music, or arranging.

## Ledger Lines

When a note falls above or below the five lines of the staff, short additional lines called **ledger lines** are used to extend the staff temporarily.

**Middle C (C4)** sits on the first ledger line below the treble clef:

```
───────────────────  E4
───────────────────  C4 (one ledger line below)
```

Common ledger line notes guitarists encounter:

| Position | Note | Guitar Location |
|----------|------|----------------|
| 1 ledger below treble | C4 (written) = C3 (sounding) | 3rd fret, A string |
| 2 ledgers below treble | A3 (written) = A2 (sounding) | Open A string |
| 1 ledger above treble | A5 (written) = A4 (sounding) | 17th fret, high E string |

## The Grand Staff

When treble and bass clefs are joined by a **brace** and connected with a middle ledger line for **C4** (middle C), they form the **grand staff**. This is the standard format for piano music and full scores. Middle C sits exactly between the two staves -- one ledger line below treble clef and one ledger line above bass clef.

## Note Names and the Musical Alphabet

Western music uses seven letter names: **A B C D E F G**. After G, the sequence repeats from A. Each letter name can be modified with **accidentals**:

- **Sharp (#):** raises a note by one semitone
- **Flat (b):** lowers a note by one semitone
- **Natural:** cancels a previous sharp or flat

These seven letters plus their sharps and flats produce the **12 chromatic pitches** that we explored with equal temperament in Level 0.

Understanding staff notation is essential for reading sheet music, communicating with other musicians, and accessing the vast library of written music available for study and performance.
