# Time Signatures & Meter

A **time signature** is the pair of numbers at the beginning of a piece that tells you how rhythm is organized into **measures** (bars). It defines both how many beats are in each measure and which note value counts as one beat.

## Reading a Time Signature

A time signature has two numbers stacked vertically:

```
Top number:     HOW MANY beats per measure
Bottom number:  WHAT NOTE VALUE gets one beat

4    =  4 beats per measure
4    =  quarter note gets 1 beat

3    =  3 beats per measure
4    =  quarter note gets 1 beat

6    =  6 beats per measure
8    =  eighth note gets 1 beat
```

The bottom number is a **code** for the note value:

| Bottom Number | Note Value |
|---------------|-----------|
| 1 | Whole note |
| 2 | Half note |
| 4 | Quarter note |
| 8 | Eighth note |
| 16 | Sixteenth note |

> **Key concept:** The bottom number is derived from the fraction: a quarter note is 1/4 of a whole note, so it is represented by 4. An eighth note is 1/8, so it is represented by 8.

## Common Time Signatures

### 4/4 (Common Time)

Four quarter-note beats per measure. This is the most common time signature in Western music -- rock, pop, blues, funk, jazz standards, and most guitar music. Often written with the symbol **C** (for "common time").

```
Beat emphasis:  STRONG - weak - medium - weak
Count:          1        2      3        4
```

### 3/4 (Waltz Time)

Three quarter-note beats per measure. Creates a **triple** feel -- the classic waltz pattern. Also common in ballads, some folk music, and many classical pieces.

```
Beat emphasis:  STRONG - weak - weak
Count:          1        2      3
```

### 6/8 (Compound Duple)

Six eighth-note beats per measure, but **felt as two groups of three**. This creates a lilting, rolling feel distinct from 3/4:

```
Counted:        1  2  3  4  5  6
Felt as:        ONE two three FOUR five six
Groups:         [  group 1  ] [  group 2  ]
```

> **3/4 vs 6/8:** Both contain the equivalent of six eighth notes per measure, but they **feel** different. In 3/4, you feel **three strong pulses** with each subdivided in two. In 6/8, you feel **two strong pulses** with each subdivided in three. Think of it as 3 x 2 versus 2 x 3.

### 2/4 and 2/2 (Cut Time)

**2/4** has two quarter-note beats per measure -- common in marches and polkas. **2/2** (cut time, written as a **C** with a vertical line through it) has two half-note beats per measure, often used when the tempo is fast and counting in quarter notes would feel overly subdivided.

## Strong and Weak Beats

Every time signature creates a pattern of **strong** (accented) and **weak** (unaccented) beats called **meter**:

```
4/4:  STRONG  weak  medium  weak     (duple with subdivision)
3/4:  STRONG  weak  weak              (triple)
6/8:  STRONG  weak  weak  medium  weak  weak   (compound duple)
2/4:  STRONG  weak                    (simple duple)
```

**Beat 1** (the **downbeat**) is always the strongest beat. On guitar, you can emphasize strong beats by strumming harder, playing bass notes on beat 1, or using accents. The pattern of strong and weak beats is what gives music its sense of forward motion and groove.

## Simple vs Compound Meter

Time signatures fall into two categories based on how each beat subdivides:

**Simple meter:** each beat divides into **two** equal parts
- 2/4, 3/4, 4/4 -- the beat (quarter note) divides into two eighth notes

**Compound meter:** each beat divides into **three** equal parts
- 6/8, 9/8, 12/8 -- each dotted-quarter beat divides into three eighth notes

```
Simple (4/4):    beat = ♩   subdivides as ♪♪      (2 equal parts)
Compound (6/8):  beat = ♩.  subdivides as ♪♪♪     (3 equal parts)
```

The mathematical distinction: in simple meter, the beat note is undotted. In compound meter, the actual felt beat is a **dotted note** (the top number divided by 3 gives the number of felt beats).

## Guitar Application: Strumming Patterns

Time signatures directly determine strumming patterns:

```
4/4 basic strum:   ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑
                   1  +  2  +  3  +  4  +

3/4 waltz strum:   ↓     ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑
                   1     2  +  3  +

6/8 flowing:       ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓
                   1  2  3  4  5  6
```

When learning a new song, always identify the time signature first. It determines the rhythmic framework that every other musical decision fits into. Tap or count along before you start playing -- establishing the meter internally before adding notes is the hallmark of a solid rhythm player.
