# Consonance & Dissonance

Why do some note combinations sound pleasant and stable while others create tension and unease? The answer lies in the **frequency ratios** between the notes -- a direct connection to the harmonic series we studied in Level 0.

## Defining the Terms

**Consonance** is the quality of intervals that sound **stable**, **blended**, and **at rest**. Consonant intervals feel like they could sustain indefinitely without needing to move anywhere.

**Dissonance** is the quality of intervals that sound **tense**, **unstable**, and **in motion**. Dissonant intervals create a pull toward resolution -- they want to move to a consonant interval.

> **Important:** Dissonance is not "bad." Music depends on the interplay of tension and release. Without dissonance, music would be static and lifeless. Without consonance, it would be relentlessly tense. The art is in balancing the two.

## The Physics of Consonance

Consonance correlates with the **simplicity of the frequency ratio** between two notes. Simpler ratios produce more consonance:

```
Interval          Ratio    Simplicity    Consonance
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Perfect unison     1:1     simplest      most consonant
Perfect octave     2:1     very simple   very consonant
Perfect fifth      3:2     simple        consonant
Perfect fourth     4:3     simple        consonant
Major third        5:4     moderate      consonant
Minor third        6:5     moderate      consonant
Major sixth        5:3     moderate      consonant
Minor sixth        8:5     complex       mildly consonant
Major second       9:8     complex       mildly dissonant
Minor seventh      16:9    complex       dissonant
Major seventh      15:8    complex       dissonant
Minor second       16:15   very complex  very dissonant
Tritone            45:32   very complex  most dissonant
```

The physical explanation: when two frequencies have a simple ratio (like 3:2), their wave peaks align frequently, producing a smooth, blended sound. When the ratio is complex (like 16:15), the waves rarely align, producing **beating** -- audible fluctuations in volume that our brain interprets as tension.

## Categories of Consonance

Historically, intervals are grouped into three categories:

### Perfect Consonances

| Interval | Semitones | Ratio | Character |
|----------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Perfect unison (P1) | 0 | 1:1 | Complete fusion |
| Perfect octave (P8) | 12 | 2:1 | Near-fusion, same note class |
| Perfect fifth (P5) | 7 | 3:2 | Open, stable, "hollow" |

Perfect consonances are so stable they were the **only** intervals considered consonant in medieval music. The perfect fifth is the most important interval in harmony -- it defines keys, roots, and the structure of chords.

### Imperfect Consonances

| Interval | Semitones | Ratio | Character |
|----------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Major third (M3) | 4 | 5:4 | Bright, warm, happy |
| Minor third (m3) | 3 | 6:5 | Dark, warm, sad |
| Major sixth (M6) | 9 | 5:3 | Warm, stable |
| Minor sixth (m6) | 8 | 8:5 | Bittersweet |

Imperfect consonances are considered stable and pleasant but have more **color** and **character** than perfect consonances. They give chords their major/minor quality.

### Dissonances

| Interval | Semitones | Ratio | Character |
|----------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Major second (M2) | 2 | 9:8 | Mild tension, "crunchy" |
| Minor second (m2) | 1 | 16:15 | Strong tension, "clashing" |
| Major seventh (M7) | 11 | 15:8 | Bright tension, "shimmering" |
| Minor seventh (m7) | 10 | 16:9 | Bluesy, pulling downward |
| Tritone (A4/d5) | 6 | 45:32 | Maximum tension, "diabolus in musica" |

> **The tritone** divides the octave exactly in half (6 of 12 semitones). It was historically called "diabolus in musica" (the devil in music) because of its extreme instability. In modern music, it is the essential tension in **dominant seventh chords** that drives harmonic resolution.

## Connection to the Harmonic Series

The consonance hierarchy maps directly to the harmonic series from Level 0:

```
Harmonic:   1    2    3    4    5    6    7    8
Note:       A2   A3   E4   A4   C#5  E5   ~G5  A5
Interval:        P8   P5   P8   M3   P5   ~m7  P8
```

The intervals that appear earliest in the harmonic series (octave at harmonic 2, fifth at harmonic 3, major third at harmonic 5) are the most consonant. The intervals that appear later or not at all are progressively more dissonant.

This is not coincidence -- when you play two notes whose frequency ratio matches the harmonic series, you are **reinforcing** the natural overtones already present in each note. Your ear recognizes this alignment as stability.

Explore frequency ratios directly. Set one oscillator to a base frequency (like 220 Hz), then tune the other to simple ratios (Octave = 2:1, Fifth = 3:2) to hear consonance. ## Tension and Resolution on Guitar

On guitar, you can feel consonance and dissonance viscerally by playing two-note combinations (**double stops**):

```
Consonant double stop (P5):
  5th fret, A string (D) + 7th fret, D string (A) = perfect fifth
  --> sounds open, stable

Dissonant double stop (m2):
  5th fret, B string (E) + 6th fret, high E string (F) = minor second
  --> sounds tense, clashing, wants to resolve

Resolution:
  Move the F down to E (both strings at 5th fret) = unison
  --> tension releases into stability
```

The cycle of **tension** (dissonance) followed by **release** (consonance) is the engine that drives music forward. Every chord progression, every melody, and every harmonic rhythm is built on this fundamental principle. Understanding which intervals create tension and which provide resolution gives you conscious control over the emotional arc of your playing.
