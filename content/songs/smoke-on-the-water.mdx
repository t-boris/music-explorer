# Smoke on the Water

One of the most recognizable riffs in rock history, "Smoke on the Water" by Deep Purple is an excellent study in **intervals** and **power chord** movement. The main riff is built entirely on **parallel fourths**, making it a perfect entry point for understanding interval relationships on the guitar.

## The Main Riff: Interval Analysis

The iconic riff uses two notes played simultaneously a **perfect fourth** apart. This interval (5 semitones) creates the characteristic "hollow" power chord sound that defines rock guitar.

The riff moves through these intervals:

```
G4-Bb4 → Bb4-Db5 → C5-Eb5 → G4-Bb4 → Bb4-Db5 → C5 → Bb4
```

Each pair of notes is a perfect fourth. The motion between chord positions outlines a **minor pentatonic** contour in G minor.

## Rhythm and Feel

The riff sits in **4/4 time** with a straight eighth-note feel. The rhythmic pattern uses a mix of quarter notes and eighth notes with rests that give the riff its signature breathing space.

Key rhythmic concepts:
- **Steady pulse** at moderate tempo (~112 BPM)
- **Rest placement** creates the "da-da-daa" phrasing
- **Consistent attack** on each double-stop

## Theory Connections

| Concept | How It Appears |
|---------|---------------|
| Perfect fourths | Every double-stop in the riff |
| Minor tonality | G minor key center |
| Power chords | Parallel interval movement |
| Pentatonic contour | Root motion follows minor pentatonic scale |

## Practice Approach

1. **Start slow** — play the interval pairs at 60 BPM, focusing on clean two-note voicings
2. **Map the intervals** — identify each perfect fourth on the fretboard
3. **Add rhythm** — once the notes are clean, incorporate the rhythmic pattern
4. **Increase tempo** — gradually work up to performance tempo (112 BPM)

> This riff demonstrates that powerful music can come from simple interval relationships. Mastering parallel fourths here prepares you for more complex voicings in later levels.
