# Come As You Are

The opening riff of "Come As You Are" by Nirvana is a masterclass in **chromatic movement** and **economy of motion**. Built around the low E and A strings, the riff uses **half-step intervals** (minor seconds) to create tension and release within an **E minor** framework.

## The Riff: Chromatic Analysis

The main riff moves stepwise through a narrow range, emphasizing **chromatic passing tones** between diatonic scale degrees:

```
E → F# → G → F# → E → F# → G → F# → A → A# → A → G → A → G → E
```

Notice the chromatic motion:
- **E to F#** — whole step (major second)
- **F# to G** — half step (minor second)
- **A to A#** — half step (chromatic approach)

The half-step movements create a sense of **restless motion** that defines the song's mood.

## Scale Context

While the song is rooted in **E minor**, the riff doesn't strictly follow the natural minor scale. The inclusion of **A#** (enharmonic Bb) acts as a **chromatic passing tone** between A and B, adding tension before resolving.

This blending of diatonic and chromatic elements is common in grunge and alternative rock.

## Technique: Clean Tone and Chorus

The riff is played with a **clean tone** and heavy **chorus effect**, which requires:
- **Even finger pressure** for consistent volume across notes
- **Smooth legato transitions** between chromatic steps
- **Controlled picking** to maintain the flowing, hypnotic quality

## Theory Connections

| Concept | How It Appears |
|---------|---------------|
| Minor seconds | Chromatic half-step motion throughout |
| Major seconds | Whole-step movements (E to F#) |
| Chromatic passing tones | A# between diatonic A and B |
| Natural minor scale | E minor as the tonal center |

## Practice Approach

1. **Play each note individually** — ensure clean fretting on the low strings
2. **Focus on the half steps** — feel the tension of chromatic motion
3. **Maintain even tempo** — use a metronome at 120 BPM
4. **Add dynamics** — keep volume consistent while varying articulation

> This riff teaches you that chromaticism can be both simple and deeply expressive. Understanding how half-step movement creates tension prepares you for modal and jazz harmony concepts in later levels.
