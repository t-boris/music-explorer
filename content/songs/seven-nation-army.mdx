# Seven Nation Army

"Seven Nation Army" by The White Stripes features one of the most memorable riffs of the 21st century. Despite its simplicity, the riff is a study in how **interval choice** and **rhythmic placement** create an unforgettable musical statement.

## The Riff: Interval Breakdown

The main riff is a single-note melody played on the low E string (with an octave pedal effect giving it a bass-like tone):

```
E → E → G → E → D → C → B
```

Interval analysis from the root (E):
- **E to E** — unison (repetition for rhythmic emphasis)
- **E to G** — minor third (3 semitones)
- **G to E** — minor third descending
- **E to D** — major second descending (whole step)
- **D to C** — major second descending (whole step)
- **C to B** — minor second descending (half step)

The riff outlines a **descending stepwise motion** from E down to B (a perfect fourth below), with the minor third jump to G providing the melodic hook.

## Rhythmic Power

What makes this riff so effective is its **rhythm**:

- The opening **E notes** are played as a **dotted quarter note** pattern, creating a syncopated feel against the 4/4 pulse
- The descending line accelerates rhythmically, building momentum
- Strategic **rests** between phrases let the riff breathe

Key rhythmic concepts:
- **Dotted rhythms** create forward momentum
- **Syncopation** against the beat gives the riff swagger
- **Silence as a tool** — the rests are as important as the notes

## Key and Tonality

The riff is in **E minor** but uses a limited pitch set. The notes E, G, D, C, B belong to both E natural minor and E minor pentatonic, demonstrating how **pentatonic thinking** simplifies melody writing.

## Theory Connections

| Concept | How It Appears |
|---------|---------------|
| Minor third | The signature E to G leap |
| Stepwise motion | Descending D → C → B |
| Minor second | The half-step C to B resolution |
| Rhythmic syncopation | Dotted rhythms against the pulse |
| Minor pentatonic | Pitch set drawn from E minor pentatonic |

## Practice Approach

1. **Learn the pitches first** — play through the notes slowly without worrying about rhythm
2. **Tap the rhythm** — clap or tap the rhythmic pattern before adding notes
3. **Combine** — put rhythm and pitches together at 60 BPM
4. **Feel the groove** — the syncopation should feel natural, not forced
5. **Build to tempo** — target 124 BPM (original tempo)

> This riff proves that music theory doesn't need to be complex to be powerful. A minor third, some stepwise motion, and strong rhythmic placement create one of the most recognizable melodies in modern rock.
