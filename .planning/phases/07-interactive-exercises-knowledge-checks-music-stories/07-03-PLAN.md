```mdx
## From the Stage

<MusicStory
  title="Pythagoras and the Blacksmith's Workshop"
  era="6th Century BCE"
  connection="The same string-length ratios Pythagoras discovered are the physics behind every note on your guitar fretboard."
>

According to ancient tradition, the Greek philosopher Pythagoras was walking past a blacksmith's forge when he noticed that different hammers produced different pitches when they struck the anvil. Intrigued, he investigated and found that the pitch was related to the weight of the hammer — simpler weight ratios produced more harmonious sounds.

He went on to experiment with a **monochord** — a single string stretched over a wooden board. By dividing the string at different points, he discovered that simple ratios produced pleasing intervals: dividing the string in half (2:1) produced an octave, at two-thirds (3:2) a perfect fifth, and at three-quarters (4:3) a perfect fourth.

This was arguably the first moment in history when someone proved that **music follows mathematical laws**. Over two thousand years later, these same ratios govern every instrument, every chord, and every melody you will ever play.

</MusicStory>
```

**Lesson 2 — "Harmonics & Overtones"**
Add after the "The Frequency Ratios That Define Music" section:

```mdx
## From the Stage

<MusicStory
  title="Jimi Hendrix and the Art of Feedback"
  era="1967"
  connection="Hendrix's revolutionary guitar sound was built on the same harmonic physics you just learned — he turned overtones and feedback into a musical vocabulary."
>

At the Monterey Pop Festival on June 18, 1967, Jimi Hendrix performed an act that changed electric guitar forever. Beyond his legendary showmanship, what made Hendrix truly revolutionary was his mastery of **harmonics and feedback**.

When Hendrix stood in front of his amplifier, the sound waves from the speaker caused his guitar strings to vibrate sympathetically — producing **feedback**. But instead of treating this as unwanted noise, Hendrix learned to control which harmonics were amplified. By changing his position relative to the speaker, adjusting his guitar's angle, and manipulating the whammy bar, he could select specific overtones from the harmonic series and sustain them indefinitely.

His technique of lightly touching the strings while playing (artificial harmonics) and using the guitar's feedback loop as an instrument within an instrument was a practical application of everything in this lesson. The "screaming" sound in songs like "The Star-Spangled Banner" at Woodstock is literally the **upper harmonics** of the guitar strings being selectively amplified through feedback.

</MusicStory>
```

**Lesson 3 — "How We Hear Pitch"**
Add after the "The Tradeoff of Equal Temperament" section:

```mdx
## From the Stage

<MusicStory
  title="The Great Tuning War"
  era="1939"
  connection="The A=440 Hz standard you use to tune your guitar was the result of decades of debate — proof that even 'obvious' musical conventions required international negotiation."
>

For centuries, there was no universal agreement on what frequency the note A should be. In the Baroque era, A was typically around 415 Hz — nearly a semitone lower than today's standard. By the 19th century, orchestras had entered an unofficial **pitch arms race**: conductors kept raising the tuning to make their ensembles sound brighter and more brilliant. Some opera houses in Italy tuned as high as A=450 Hz, which strained singers' voices and caused real physical harm.

In 1939, an international conference in London finally standardized **A4 = 440 Hz** as the reference pitch. Even this was a compromise — the French had been pushing for 435 Hz (their national standard since 1859), while many German and Austrian orchestras preferred 443-445 Hz. The 440 Hz standard was proposed as a middle ground that could satisfy everyone.

Today, 440 Hz is nearly universal, but the debate never fully ended. Many period-instrument ensembles still tune to 415 Hz for Baroque music, and some modern orchestras (notably in Vienna and Berlin) tune to 442-443 Hz for a slightly brighter sound. Every time you tune your guitar to A=440, you are participating in a convention that took centuries of argument to establish.

</MusicStory>
```
